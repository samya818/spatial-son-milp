Foundations & Vision
Foreword: Why A3 Offset as the Single Lever
The chosen approach focuses on the A3 offset as the sole control lever. This is a standard simplification, legitimate and well documented in both academic and industrial SON literature.

📚 What the literature says

The foundational work of Wang et al. (2015) on SON optimization via Q-Learning explicitly models the A3 offset as "the primary control parameter for mobility load balancing". Zhang et al. (2023) confirm in their IEEE survey: "A3 offset is the most influential parameter for handover-triggered load balancing because it directly controls the spatial boundary of cell coverage."

The 3GPP TS 36.331 standard defines the A3 event as the standard inter-cell handover mechanism. In commercial SON deployments (Cisco MEO, Ericsson ENM, Huawei SON), the operator configures:

  • Time-to-Trigger = 320 ms (standard value, fixed)
  • Hysteresis = 1 dB (standard value, fixed)
  • FilterCoefficient = fc4 (fixed)

And optimizes only the A3 offset as a dynamic control variable. Why? Because the other parameters have complex side effects (ping-pong, call drops) and are locked by operational playbooks. The A3 offset is the only lever that the operator allows SON to modify in real time without major business risk.

💡
Distinction Between Control Simplification and Modeling Simplification

It is crucial to distinguish the complexity of the radio environment from the simplicity of the control lever. Not dynamically modeling transmit power or TTT does not mean ignoring their existence, but rather considering them as constants fixed by the standard. We thus optimize the only variable that the operator can actually vary in real time.

📖
Academic references supporting this choice

• Wang et al. (2015) — "A Q-Learning Approach for Self-Organizing Network with A3 Offset Control", IEEE TNSM. Models A3 as the sole action variable.
• Zhang et al. (2023) — "SON Load Balancing Survey", IEEE Access. Classifies A3 offset as primary parameter, TTT/hysteresis as secondary fixed parameters.
• Huang et al. (2024) — "MILP Load Balancing with Handover Offset", Computer Networks. Exact MILP formulation with A3 as the single binary variable per antenna.
• 3GPP TS 36.331 — LTE RRC Specification. Defines the A3 event: "Mn + Ofn + Ocn - Hys > Ms + Ofs + Ocs + Off", where Off is the optimizable offset.

🎯 Synthesis of the approach

Using MILP with A3 offset is the optimal mathematical tool for this problem because:

  • The problem is discrete combinatorial by nature.
  • The relationship between offset and offloaded volume is deterministic after spatial discretization.
  • The capacity constraints are linear thanks to precalculations.
  • Global coupling is ensured by exact linear constraints.

Building on hypotheses H1-H3, the complex network dynamics problem is translated into a discrete geometry and mixed-integer linear optimization problem.

⚠️
The capacity duality: two thresholds, two roles

There are two different capacities in the pipeline. Never confuse them.

1. C_a^radio (capacity_mo) = effective operational capacity (~407 MB for a macro): this is the hard operational threshold, used as constraint C_a in the MILP (C3). Exceeding this threshold means packet loss.

2. C_a^nom (nominal_capacity) = 90th percentile of historical traffic: this is the statistical trigger that decides whether to invoke the MILP. Logic: if pred_q80 > nominal_capacity → we suspect congestion → we launch the MILP with C_a = capacity_mo (physical). This avoids unnecessarily launching the MILP at every slot.

🗺️
Why does one antenna cover several cells?

An antenna (especially macro) has a coverage radius of ~800 m. A cell is ~235 m. So a macro naturally covers several adjacent cells (typically 4 to 16). These cells are neighboring and contiguous — this is the very definition of a coverage zone. Assignment is done by nearest neighbor: each cell is assigned to the antenna whose center is closest to the cell's center.

Foundations — Validated Approximations
Modeling Hypotheses & Their Justifications
Each hypothesis is an approximation. The important thing is not that it be perfect, but that it be conservative (does not underestimate risk) and locally credible (valid at the scale of a cell).
📐
H1 — Uniform consumption per user
All users in the same cell consume v_c = V_c / U_c [MB/30min/user]
▾
## Justification H1
Why this hypothesis is acceptable:

We do not know the individual profile of each user. But at the scale of a cell of ~5 hectares and a few dozen/hundred users, the law of large numbers applies weakly. Individual heterogeneity attenuates when aggregated.

More important: this hypothesis is conservative for the MILP. If in reality some users consume more than the average, the actual offloaded volume will be slightly different, but the order of magnitude remains correct. The MILP aims to move the right order of magnitude of volume (e.g.: offload 150 MB from a saturated macro), not to predict pixel-by-pixel.

Reference: Huang et al. (2024) use the same hypothesis in their MILP formulation: "We assume uniform traffic demand per user within a cell to derive the offloading fraction."
📐
H2 — Uniform user distribution across the cell
The U_c users are uniformly distributed over the cell surface
▾
## Justification H2
This is the role of cell granularity.

If each cell is small enough (your 100×100 grid over Milan ≈ 235m), the uniformity hypothesis becomes locally credible. A cell of ~5 hectares does not contain both a giant shopping mall and a deserted park. It typically contains a homogeneous block of buildings, a portion of street, or a small park.

Uniformity per cell is a much more robust approximation than uniformity per antenna (which covers km² and mixes shopping mall + park + highway).

Note: If a cell contains a hotspot (e.g., a very busy café), the error will be localized to that cell. But since handover goes to the closest neighboring antenna, the error remains geographically coherent — café users will migrate to the antenna behind the café, which is physically plausible.
📐
H3 — Signal quality = f(distance) only
Path-loss: Q_a(p) = −3.76 × log₁₀(d(a,p))
▾
## Justification H3
Macroscopic approximation validated by 3GPP.

In urban reality, signal also depends on obstacles, fading, reflections. But for coverage planning, the industry exclusively uses distance-based path-loss models:

• Okumura-Hata: PL = 69.55 + 26.16 log₁₀(f) − 13.82 log₁₀(h_b) − a(h_m) + [44.9 − 6.55 log₁₀(h_b)] log₁₀(d)
• COST-231 Hata (dense urban version)
• 3GPP TR 38.901: 3D-UMa, 3D-UMi models

Our exponent 3.76 is derived from the UMi (Urban Micro) 3GPP model for frequencies ~2 GHz and antenna heights 5–25 m. This is a macroscopic approximation: it does not predict the exact signal at a street corner, but it correctly predicts which antenna dominates which zone — which is exactly what the MILP needs.

Conservative consequence: A few users will migrate to the "wrong" antenna (due to an unmodeled building), but the majority will migrate to the right one. The MILP does not aim for pixel-perfect accuracy. It aims to move the right order of magnitude of volume. A few % error on exact location is acceptable for congestion.
📐
H4 — A3 Offset as the sole control lever
We do not model power, TTT, or hysteresis
▾
## Justification H4
Control simplification, not physical modeling simplification.

In practice, the operator sets:
• Time-to-Trigger = 320 ms (3GPP standard)
• Hysteresis = 1 dB (standard)
• FilterCoefficient = fc4

And optimizes only the A3 offset as the SON control variable. This is valid: you reduce the problem to a single lever per antenna, which is exactly what commercial SON algorithms do (Cisco MEO, Ericsson ENM, Huawei SON).

Why the other parameters are locked:
• Transmit power: modifying power affects the entire coverage, creates interference on neighboring cells, and is regulated (wave exposure). This is a planning lever, not operations.
• Time-to-Trigger: a TTT too short = ping-pongs (repeated handovers) = call drops. A TTT too long = delayed congestion response. Operators fix it at 320 ms and do not touch it.
• Hysteresis: fixed at 1 dB to avoid oscillations. This is a robustness parameter, not an optimization parameter.

Reference: 3GPP TS 36.331, section 5.5.4.4: "The UE shall trigger the A3 event when the RSRP of the neighbour cell becomes better than the RSRP of the serving cell by the offset configured by a3-Offset." The offset is the only parameter configurable by SON in real time.

Behind the Scenes — Rigor & Exploration
The Engineering Journey: What You Don't See
The final solution is the culmination of a long exploration process. We present here the technical challenges and branches explored that forged the solidity of the current system.

1. The "Model War": Beyond Boosting

Before adopting XGBoost and TiDE, we conducted deep benchmarks on alternative models:

  • SARIMA Case Studies: We tested the statistical rigor of SARIMA on isolated cells (benchmark_sarima_case_study.py). While accurate on linear trends, its per-cell computational cost (several minutes) made it unusable for 1024 cells in real time.
  • The Meta Prophet Approach: We explored Prophet (eval_prophet.py) for its native management of holidays and events. However, pure Machine Learning (Boosting) proved more responsive to the unpredictable traffic spikes of the Milan dataset.
🔬
The Obsession with Mass Conservation

A major challenge was ensuring that traffic offloading did not create "ghost" bytes or make them disappear. We developed dedicated diagnostic tools (debug_h_matrices.py) to audit each spatial transfer matrix. Every byte leaving an antenna is tracked until its arrival at a neighbor, ensuring a physically irreproachable simulation.

2. The Story of the Pivot: From 10,000 to 1,024 Cells

The project began with the ambition to cover the entire city of Milan (10,000 cells). The legacy scripts (phase1_ingestion_10k.py) bear witness to this phase. We quickly realized that for global optimization (MILP), geographic contiguity was more important than raw area. Switching to a dense 1024-cell block allowed for true cascade transfer simulation, where a scattered sample failed physically.

💡
The Refinement of Nominal Capacity

One of the project's most elegant ideas was not using a fixed capacity. By analyzing historical data, we created a Statistical Nominal Capacity (based on the 90th percentile per time slot). This allows the SON system to be "intelligent": it doesn't alert unnecessarily at night if traffic is low, but becomes extremely vigilant during peak hours.

Identified problem — Symmetric loss

The current MSE loss penalizes over- and under-estimation equally. Business-wise: underestimating congestion costs 5–10× more: the network saturates, calls drop, SLAs are violated. Correction in Phase 5: asymmetric quantile loss.

🗺️
The real journey: what we tried before getting here

The results of this phase 4 only tell part of the story. In parallel, we tested three other model families, each for a specific reason — and each rejected for a specific reason.

Prophet (Meta) handles multiple seasonality and holidays well, but it treats each cell independently, is designed for long and regular series, and reacts slowly to sudden spikes. On 600 cells in parallel, it was too slow.

SARIMA is statistically rigorous for univariate series with seasonality. We tested SARIMA(p,d,q)×(P,D,Q)₄₈ (m=48 slots = 24h). It gives good results on a single cell, but it is blind to spatial correlations between neighbors, and its per-cell computational cost makes it unworkable at the scale of 600 or 1024 simultaneous cells.

The LSTM from Phase 4 produced an MAE of 491.62 — worse than naive persistence. This is not a surprise: LSTMs suffer from vanishing gradient on long series (62 days × 48 slots = 2976 points), and require careful architectural tuning that falls outside the scope of this project.

These three paths were documented and preserved in research/. They are an integral part of the scientific process.

🔴
The failure of 600 cells: a fundamental lesson

With our first models, we applied the MILP pipeline on 600 cells selected across the entire Milan grid. The predictions were correct — but the impact on congestion was virtually zero. The reason is geographical: these 600 cells were scattered across the 100×100 grid with no spatial continuity. A saturated cell in the North had no supervised neighbor available to absorb its traffic. Offloading "into the void" solves nothing physically.

The solution was to switch to a dense contiguous block of 32×32 = 1024 cells, extracted from the center of Milan. In this block, every antenna is surrounded by supervised neighbors. Offloading could finally operate for real, unlocking the 73.53% congestion reduction.

Why TiDE and not LSTM / PatchTST?

According to 2024 benchmarks (Long-term Time Series Forecasting Benchmark), TiDE (Time-series Dense Encoder) outperforms PatchTST on 55% of long datasets and N-BEATS on 60% of short datasets. TiDE is a pure MLP without attention, faster than Transformer, natively handles past and future covariates, and converges better on heterogeneous tabular data like ours. The LSTM from Phase 4 failed (MAE=491) due to vanishing gradient on long series — TiDE does not have this problem.

Reference: Das et al. (2023), "Long-term Forecasting with TiDE", + 2024 benchmarks showing TiDE better than PatchTST on ETTm1, ETTm2, Exchange.

## Markdown Cell — Section A Header
# 05 — ML Predictor Improvements

This notebook improves the Phase 4 predictor without modifying previous notebooks.
It reads features_target_600cells.parquet and produces 3 XGBoost quantile models + 1 LightGBM corrector.

## Section A — Why asymmetric loss?
Symmetric MSE penalizes over-estimation and under-estimation equally. In this project, underestimating congestion costs 5–10× more: the network saturates, calls drop, SLAs are violated. We use the quantile loss α=0.80 which penalizes under-estimations 4× more heavily.

We train 3 models: optimistic (α=0.20), nominal (α=0.50), pessimistic (α=0.80). The width of the interval measures uncertainty. Target coverage: 78–82%.
⛔
CRITIQUE — XGBoost Syntax ≠ LightGBM (silent confusion)

Both compile without error but give radically different results.

⚠️
Silent syntax trap: XGBoost vs LightGBM

In XGBoost, quantile regression is activated with objective='reg:quantileerror' and quantile_alpha=0.80. In LightGBM, it's objective='quantile' and alpha=0.80. Mixing the two conventions produces no error — but the model silently trains on a wrong objective. Empirical validation of the [q20, q80] interval coverage is the only way to detect this bug.

## What this section does in the notebook
We train three distinct XGBoost models, each targeting a different quantile of future traffic. The q20 (optimistic) model estimates the floor: traffic will likely be above. The q50 (median) model estimates the central value: it is the most neutral estimator. The q80 (pessimistic) model estimates the probable ceiling: in 80% of cases, actual traffic will be below this prediction.

It is the q80 that we use to trigger the MILP. We prefer to slightly overestimate future congestion and act with caution rather than underestimate it and arrive too late.

The three models share the same architecture (n_estimators=500, learning_rate=0.05, max_depth=6, subsample=0.8) and the same train/test split: days 1 to 56 for training, days 57 to 62 for evaluation. At the end, we measure the empirical coverage of the [q20, q80] interval, which must be between 78% and 82% for the model to be correctly calibrated. The three models are serialized to disk to be loaded directly by the MILP engine and the closed-loop simulator.

Section B — Past error features + L2 Stacking
error_t1, error_mean_6, error_sign · LightGBM residual corrector
▾
## What this section does in the notebook
Why a second model? XGBoost q50 makes a good global prediction, but it commits systematic errors on certain cell profiles — for example, it regularly underestimates antennas in commercial zones on Friday evenings.

The principle of L2 stacking is simple: after the first model (L1 = XGBoost q50) has predicted, we compute the residual residual = actual − predicted. A second model (L2 = LightGBM) is then trained not on raw traffic, but on these residuals. It learns to correct L1's recurrent errors.

The final prediction is the sum final_prediction = XGBoost_q50 + LightGBM_correction. So that L2 has contextual information, we feed it all original features plus L1's prediction.

We use LightGBM with a standard regression objective (regression), because here we are no longer seeking to estimate a quantile but the optimal correction in expectation. The typical gain from this stacking is 3 to 8% MAE reduction on difficult patterns. The corrector model is saved separately as lgbm_l2_corrector.pkl.

neuralforecast · TiDE (Time-series Dense Encoder)

## What this section does in the notebook
Before adopting XGBoost as the main model, we explored several alternatives.

SARIMA was our first attempt. It is a classical statistical model for time series with seasonality. We tested SARIMA(p,d,q)×(P,D,Q)₄₈ (seasonality of 48 slots = 24h). Results were correct for daily trends, but SARIMA treats each cell completely independently, is slow to train (several minutes per cell), and does not capture spatial correlations between neighbors. On 600 cells, the computational cost was prohibitive.

Prophet (Meta) was our second attempt. It handles multiple seasonality (daily + weekly) and holidays well, but it is designed for long and regular series. On our telecom data with sudden spikes, its reaction capacity was insufficient. Moreover, Prophet is designed for cell-by-cell use — its parallelism is difficult to exploit properly.

TiDE (Time-series Dense Encoder) is a neural model that we integrated for comparison. It uses an MLP architecture with dense encoders and residual connections. It differs from LSTM (which suffers from vanishing gradient on long series) by its ability to model long-distance dependencies without costly attention mechanisms. Here, TiDE receives 96 slots of history (48h), the 5 cyclical covariates (sin/cos of hour, sin/cos of day, is_weekend), and predicts 2 slots ahead (= 1h). It is trained in global mode on all cells simultaneously, which makes it much better than SARIMA or Prophet at generalizing.

In production, XGBoost q80 + LightGBM corrector was retained as it offers the best trade-off between accuracy, inference speed, and feature interpretability.

Section D — Calibrated nominal capacity
90th percentile by (time window × day type) · 12 thresholds per cell
▾
## What this section does in the notebook
Why a "nominal" capacity and not a fixed capacity? An antenna's traffic in a residential zone at 3 AM has nothing to do with that of a commercial zone antenna at 6 PM. Using a single capacity for all situations would be too restrictive during off-peak hours and insufficient during peak hours.

We divide each day into 4 time windows: night (0–6 AM), morning (6 AM–12 PM), afternoon (12 PM–7 PM), evening (7 PM–midnight). We also distinguish 2 day types: weekday and weekend. This gives 8 combinations per cell. For each combination, the nominal capacity is set to the 90th percentile of historically observed traffic. In other words: the capacity is the volume that the cell rarely exceeds (1 in 10 times) under normal conditions.

These thresholds are stored in nominal_capacities.parquet and used by the MILP engine to decide whether an antenna is "congested" at a given moment. If the q80 prediction exceeds the nominal capacity of the current slot, the antenna is flagged as an offloading candidate.

## What this section does in the notebook
Each physical antenna in the model has a radio profile defined by three parameters: bandwidth, target SINR, and transmit power. The effective antenna capacity is calculated via the Shannon formula: C = Bandwidth × log₂(1 + linear_SINR) × spectral_efficiency × utilization_rate × sharing_factor.

The sharing_factor of 0.05 is crucial: it reflects the fact that the antenna serves about 20 users simultaneously and that the cell covers only a fraction of the physical antenna's sectorized zone. Without this factor, the raw Shannon formula would give ~8,000 MB/30min — completely disconnected from the real Milan dataset traffic.

We define three antenna profiles calibrated on standard urban LTE deployments:
— Macro: 20 MHz band, target SINR 15 dB, power 46 dBm, height 25m, coverage radius ~3.5 cells. Capacity: ~407 MB/30min.
— Micro: 20 MHz band, target SINR 20 dB (better signal-to-noise ratio due to proximity), power 37 dBm, height 8m. Capacity: ~539 MB/30min.
— Small cell: 10 MHz band, SINR 25 dB, power 24 dBm, height 4m. Capacity: ~337 MB/30min.

These profiles are placed with variable densities depending on the geographic zone in the 100×100 grid representing Milan: the city center (rows 40–60, columns 40–60) is saturated with micros and small cells, the urban ring alternates macros and micros, and the periphery is covered almost exclusively by macros.

Cell B — Antenna placement + 10,000-cell assignment
Zone-based generation · vectorized assignment · 600 subset extraction

## What this section does in the notebook
Once the profiles are defined, we physically place the antennas in the 100×100 grid. Each density zone generates a number of antennas proportional to its area and deployment density (for example, the center has a density of 0.22 antenna per square cell, vs. 0.012 in the periphery). Positions are drawn randomly within the zone, with slight gaussian jitter to avoid overly regular configurations. A minimum distance constraint (0.5 cell) prevents two antennas from being too close.

Once antennas are placed, we assign the 10,000 geographic cells to their physically closest antenna, using a KD-Tree algorithm (data structure enabling nearest neighbor search in O(log n)). The geometric center of each cell in the 100×100 grid is compared to the position of all antennas, and each cell is affiliated with the closest antenna in Euclidean distance.

For simulations, we then extract the subset of 1024 cells forming the dense 32×32 block (rows 34–65, columns 34–65 of the grid), and the antennas covering this block constitute our working graph. This block contains 201 antennas with perfect topological continuity — every antenna is surrounded by neighbors, which makes offloading possible in all directions.

## What this section does in the notebook
The Telecom Italia Milan dataset records aggregated traffic volumes (Internet, SMS, calls) per cell and per 30-minute slot. It does not contain the number of active users at each instant. Yet, the handover model depends on the number of users likely to switch — not just on volume.

Spatial Simulator — Offline Precalculation
Core of the architectural change. For each cell and each level δ ∈ {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0} dB, calculate exactly what surface fraction switches to which neighboring antenna (physical path-loss). This calculation is done once per day offline.
🔬
Physical principle: why replace fixed θ and linear f(δ)?

Fixed θ was wrong: users at the North of a cell switch to the North antenna, those at the East to the East — it is not a global percentage. Linear f(δ) was wrong: it is an S-curve (sigmoid). Solution: discretize each cell into a 50×50 grid, calculate for each point its distance to antennas, derive its quality (path-loss = −3.76 × log₁₀(distance)), and count the points that switch. With cells of equal surface, no need to normalize by area — volume is proportional to the number of points.

SpatialTransferSimulator · Vectorized NumPy · 50×50 grid

## Markdown Cell — Header
# 08 — Spatial Simulator (Offline Precalculation)

This notebook calculates the transfer fractions for each cell. For each point of a 50×50 grid inside a cell, we calculate signal quality from each antenna (path-loss = −3.76 × log₁₀(distance)). When the master lowers its threshold by δ dB, points whose best neighbor exceeds the master by δ switch.

This calculation is independent of traffic — only of geometry. It is done once per day (offline, 10–30 seconds) and stored. ML predictions inject volume online.
## What this section does in the notebook
This is the most central calculation of the project — the one that translates the physical geometry of the network into mathematical coefficients usable by the MILP.

For each geographic cell, we discretize its surface into a 50×50 = 2500 point grid. For each point of this micro-grid, we calculate the received signal quality from the master antenna and from all its neighboring antennas. Quality is modeled by the standard 3GPP path-loss: Q = −3.76 × log₁₀(distance). A signal decreases with distance according to this exponent of 3.76, typical of a dense urban environment.

For an A3 offset of δ dB, a point switches from its master antenna to a neighbor if and only if the neighbor's quality is at least δ dB higher than the master's. By counting the number of points that switch (and to which neighbor), we obtain the transfer fraction: if 12.5% of the 2500 points of a cell switch to antenna B for δ=1.5 dB, then 12.5% of this cell's traffic is transferred to B when an offset of 1.5 dB is applied.

This calculation is performed for the 7 offset levels {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0 dB} and for all cells of the 1024 block. It is performed only once, offline (in 10 to 30 seconds), and stored in fractions_1024.parquet. At each decision slot, the MILP engine only needs to read these precalculated fractions — without ever recalculating path-loss. This is what makes the real-time system viable.

✓ Validated — Decision Engine 09_milp_decision_engine.ipynb · src/optimization/milp_engine.py
MILP Engine — Decision Optimization
The heart of the system. This engine simultaneously solves the congestion problem for all antennas. It chooses the optimal A3 offset for each antenna while guaranteeing that offloaded traffic toward a neighbor does not saturate that neighbor. This ensures global optimality compared to simple heuristic approaches.
🔗
Constraint (C3) is the most critical — global coupling

If antenna A offloads to B, and B also offloads, the final volume on B = V_B − offloaded_B + received from A (and all others). This constraint is global and prevents an antenna from being overloaded by its neighbors' offloading. This is what the simplified LP ignored.

⚖️
Capacity duality: radio (topology) vs statistical (nominal_capacities)

There are two different capacities in the pipeline. capacity_mo in network_topology.yaml = effective operational capacity (~407 MB for a macro): this is the hard operational threshold, used as constraint C_a in the MILP (C3). nominal_capacity in nominal_capacities.parquet = 90th percentile of historically observed traffic by time window: this is the statistical trigger that decides whether to invoke the MILP. Logic: if pred_q80 > nominal_capacity → we suspect congestion → we launch the MILP with C_a = capacity_mo (physical). This avoids unnecessarily launching the MILP at every slot.

## Markdown Cell — Header
# 09 — MILP Decision Engine

This notebook implements the congestion optimizer. It takes as input ML predictions (forecasted volume per cell) and precalculated fractions (Phase 8), and decides which A3 threshold to apply to each antenna.

Why MILP and not LP? The transfer function H(δ) is non-linear (S-curve). By discretizing δ into 7 levels, we transform the continuous choice into a discrete choice among precalculated states. Each antenna chooses exactly one level k → binary variable z_{a,k}. Solvable with optimality guarantee in 2–10 seconds for 150 antennas.
## What this section does in the notebook
The MILP engine starts by building two three-dimensional matrices from the precalculated fractions.

The H_offload matrix represents the fraction of antenna A's traffic that leaves when an offset of level k is applied: H_offload[a, k] = fraction of V_a that leaves A at offset level k.

The H_recv matrix represents the volume received by antenna B from A for level k: H_recv[b, a, k] = fraction of V_a that B receives when A activates offset k. These matrices are built by iterating over the fractions file and multiplying each fraction by the predicted volume V_a.

These two matrices are the only inputs the solver needs to compute the optimal solution. They encode all the physics of the network (path-loss, neighbor topology, cell geometry) in a linear form directly usable by a standard MILP solver.
## What this section does in the notebook
The MILP formulation itself is defined with Pyomo, a Python mathematical optimization framework compatible with many solvers (CBC, Gurobi, GLPK).

The decision variables are the discrete offset levels: for each antenna A, an integer variable x[a] takes a value in {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6} corresponding to levels {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0 dB}.

The objective function minimizes the sum of unsatisfied volume across all antennas. The unsatisfied volume of antenna B is defined as: max(0, resulting_volume_on_B − capacity_of_B). Since we cannot have a non-linear max() in a linear MILP, we introduce slack variables s[b] ≥ 0 and constrain s[b] ≥ resulting_volume_on_B − capacity_of_B.

The constraints ensure volume conservation: all traffic that leaves A necessarily arrives at its neighbors — nothing is created or destroyed. They also ensure that a non-congested antenna keeps its offset at 0 (no unnecessary offloading that would destabilize the network).

For 200 antennas, the problem has ~200 integer variables and ~400 constraints. The CBC solver solves it in under 2 seconds, which is perfectly compatible with a 30-minute decision cycle.

10_monitoring.ipynb — Drift detection + anomalies
STL residual as anomaly score · Page-Hinkley test · Rolling MAE
▾
## What this section does in the notebook
The drift monitor continuously watches prediction quality to detect a change in network behavior that would make the ML model obsolete.

The mechanics are based on a rolling window of 48 slots (24h). At each slot, the absolute error |predicted − actual| is calculated and compared to the distribution of recent errors. A point is declared an anomaly if its STL residual exceeds 2 times the standard deviation of the current window's errors. The STL residual is the difference between the observed value and the trend+seasonality component decomposed by the STL algorithm.

For a drift alert to be triggered (rather than a simple isolated spike), 5 consecutive anomalies are required. This mechanism avoids false positives on one-off events like a football match or a local outage.

The monitor also calculates decisional precision: what percentage of slots did the model correctly predict the congested or non-congested state of a cell, regardless of absolute volume error? This is the metric that directly drives MILP decision quality, because a MILP triggered on a false alarm consumes resources unnecessarily.

## What this section does in the notebook
The closed loop is the final simulation that validates the entire pipeline under realistic conditions. It runs on days 57 to 62 of the dataset (48 slots × 6 days = 288 decisions).

At each 30-minute slot:
1. The XGBoost q80 model predicts traffic for each cell for the next hour.
2. Predictions are aggregated at the physical antenna scale (sum of cells covered by each antenna).
3. For each antenna, the prediction is compared to its nominal capacity. If the prediction exceeds capacity, the antenna is an offloading candidate.
4. If candidate antennas exist, the MILP engine is invoked. It calculates the optimal offset vector in ~2 seconds.
5. Offsets are applied via the precalculated transfer matrices: traffic is numerically redistributed between antennas.
6. The resulting traffic is compared to each antenna's capacity and remaining unsatisfied volume is counted.

Three policies are compared in parallel on the same data:
— Static: no SON action, we simply measure raw congestion.
— Conservative (XGBoost q50 + MILP): moderate triggering, fewer false positives.
— Pessimistic (XGBoost q80 + MILP): early triggering, maximizes prevention.

Over 48 test slots (24h equivalent), the final results are: static unsatisfied volume = 160,237 MB, dynamic unsatisfied volume (q80) = 42,419 MB, representing a reduction of 73.53%. Mass conservation is verified to <1e-9 precision at each slot.

✓ Validated — Architecture docs/adr/
Architecture Decision Records (ADR)
History of major architectural decisions made during the project development.
Three key decisions structure the current project:
  1. ADR-001: Quantile Loss Choice
    Abandonment of MSE (Mean Squared Error) for XGBoost in favor of a quantile loss at α=0.8. Reason: underestimating congestion is more costly for the operator than overestimating.
  2. ADR-002: Global MILP Optimization
    Use of a global MILP solver to coordinate offloading decisions. Reason: only a global approach guarantees that offloading from one antenna does not saturate its neighbors (coupling problem).
  3. ADR-003: Switch to 32×32 Dense Block
    Major pivot abandoning random sampling for a contiguous block of 1024 cells. Reason: spatial contiguity is essential to simulate realistic handovers and obtain a significant congestion gain.
